## Starter maintenance (תחזוקת מחמצת)

- **Fridge storage** is the default when not baking daily. Cold slows the microbes
  and spaces out feeds. Jar with a non-airtight lid (let gases escape).
- **Feed frequency:** at least once a week; every two weeks can work at low bake
  frequency, but watch its state.
- **Weekly refresh (not baking):** discard most, keep ~20–30 g, add equal flour +
  water (1:1:1), mix, leave at room temp until doubled (**3–5 h**), return to
  fridge.
- **Pre-bake refresh** is the hardest part to time — see the table in
  [`timing-model.md` §8](./timing-model.md). Example to make 150 g active starter
  (use 100 g, keep 50 g mother) at 1:1:1: 50 g starter + 50 g flour + 50 g water,
  warmish water (25–28 °C), mark the level, cover loosely, hold 22–28 °C.
- **Ready signs:** doubled+, airy bubbles, top slightly domed or flat. If it's
  begun to collapse it's slightly past peak but still usable. Time to peak
  **3–12 h** depending on temp + ratio + strength.
- **Flour:** white bread or all-purpose. Don't change flour type early on.
- **Water:** Israeli tap water is fine (low chlorine). Mineral water optional,
  negligible effect.
- **Smell:** healthy = mild sour, yogurt/fruit notes. Sharp acetone/alcohol
  ("hooch") = very hungry. Rot/vomit smell = possible contamination.
- **Hooch:** dark liquid layer when hungry or long in fridge (alcohol + water).
  Stir back in, or pour off if abundant/sharp.
- **Mold** (pink/green/black/orange) → discard all, start over. Don't risk it.
- Hygiene: always clean jars/tools. Flexibility: it's a living thing, adjust.

## Scheduling logic (תזמון — anchor on T)

The whole schedule is built around **T = start of mix/knead**, the moment the
starter must be at peak. Four key stages: refresh starter → knead + folds → shape
→ bake.

- **Shape** happens **T + 4 to 6 h** (this is the bulk window). After shaping →
  cold retard → bake is flexible.
- **Refresh timing:** to have the starter peak at T, feed at **T − X**, where X is
  read from the temp × ratio table. Ratio is the lever — higher ratio = longer.
  *Example:* mix at 07:00, room 22 °C, ratio 1:2:2 → X = 9 h → feed at 22:00 prior.
- **Bake** is flexible: 8–24 h (often more) after shaping, straight from the
  fridge.

## Kneading & gluten (לישה)

**Gluten = the dough's skeleton.** Two flour proteins:
- **Glutenin** → strength + elasticity (resists stretch, springs back).
- **Gliadin** → extensibility (stretches without tearing).
Kneading aligns and links them into a 3D net that traps CO₂ from fermentation →
rise and open crumb.

- **Under-kneaded:** weak net, dough spreads, dense heavy low-volume loaf.
- **Over-kneaded:** (only possible by machine, never by hand) tears the gluten
  bonds, dough turns sticky/slack, flat loaf, pale crust, also oxidizes
  carotenoids hurting color/flavor.

### Autolyse
Pre-knead rest of **flour + water only** (no salt, no starter in the classic
form). Hydrates the flour passively and starts enzyme activity:
- **Proteases** gently cut protein bonds → more extensible, easier to knead.
- **Amylases** break starch into simple sugars → food for the microbes later.
- **Duration 20–60 min** at room temp; whole/bran-rich flours benefit from longer
  (up to 2 h+, sometimes in fridge). Avoid over-long warm autolyse (weakens
  gluten). **Salt always added after** — it slows enzymes and tightens gluten.
- Benefits: shorter active knead, smoother dough, better flavor, better oven
  spring + crust. More relevant for machine kneading than hand.

### Knead types
- **Mixer:** dough hook. Low speed 1–2 min to combine, then low-medium until
  developed. Dough climbs the hook and clears the bowl. Watch for windowpane.
  **Ideal finish dough temp 24–26 °C**; above 28–29 °C harms the culture. Friction
  heats the dough — use cooler water if needed. Typically 5–15 min.
- **Hand:** combine until no dry flour, then build strength through **folds during
  bulk** rather than long kneading. Wet hands, not extra flour, for sticky dough.

## Bulk fermentation + folds (באלק)

The most important stage for flavor, aroma, and structure — between end of mix and
divide/shape.

### Why fold
- Strengthens and organizes the gluten net gradually (no aggressive kneading).
- Distributes temperature, microbes, and food evenly for uniform fermentation.
- Degasses gently, regulating fermentation and developing complex flavor.

### Fold techniques
- **Stretch & fold:** wet hands, grab far edge, stretch up to light resistance
  (don't tear), fold over center; rotate bowl 90°, repeat all four sides = one set
  of 4.
- **Coil fold:** for very wet dough (>75–80% hydration). Wet both hands, lift the
  whole mass from the middle, let the near end tuck under, set down so the far end
  folds beneath; rotate 180°, repeat. Builds vertical tension, keeps max gas.

### Duration & timing
**No fixed time.** Driven by:
- **Temperature** (biggest factor) — *dough* temp ideal **24–26 °C** (not room
  temp). Cooler → 8–12 h+; warmer → 2–3 h.
- **Starter % + activity** — more active starter shortens it.
- **Flour** — whole/rye ferment faster (more enzymes + food).
- **Hydration** — wetter ferments slightly faster.
- **Fold schedule:** 3–4 sets in the first ~2 h, intervals 15–45 min — more
  frequent early (weak dough), spaced out or stopped as it strengthens.

### End signs (read the dough, not the clock)
- Volume up **+30% to +75%** (NOT necessarily doubled). Clear bowl + start mark
  helps.
- Airy, light, alive to the touch; bubbles of varied sizes on top and sides.
- Smooth, slightly taut, domed surface; rounded convex edges where dough meets
  bowl = enough internal gas pressure.
- Pleasant, noticeable but gentle sour smell. Sharp vinegar/alcohol = over-fermenting.
- **"Under-proofed is better than over-proofed."** Over-proof → slack, sticky,
  weak gluten, collapses in the oven, flat dense over-sour loaf.

## Divide & pre-shape (חלוקה ועיצוב מקדים)

The most technical stage — small angle changes make big differences.

### Divide
- Lightly flour the bench; scale + bench scraper. Pour the dough out preserving
  airiness. Clean, fast cuts — **don't saw** (tears gluten). Weigh for uniformity,
  minimize re-cuts. **Minimum pressure** (degassing hurts crumb). Work fast but
  gently to keep temp + structure.

### Pre-shape
- **Why:** organize the messy gluten into a basic loose ball, start gentle surface
  tension, prep for final shape, let gluten relax.
- **How:** bench lightly floured or bare (need some friction). Cut side up, fold
  edges to center like closing a sack, flip smooth-side up. Loose ball, not too
  tight.
- **Bench rest:** cover, rest **15–60 min** (depends on room temp + dough
  strength).
- Notes: controlled friction builds tension (too much flour → slips, no tension;
  too little on sticky dough → sticks and tears). Angle of force matters (slightly
  down + diagonally inward toward the base) to build a "skin." The rest relaxes the
  contracted gluten so it won't resist or tear in final shaping.

## Final shaping (עיצוב סופי)

- **Why:** set the loaf shape (boule / batard), build a **strong elastic skin** for
  the final proof + oven spring + crust, and even out the crumb.
- **Stitching technique:** smooth ("pretty") side down on lightly floured bench.
  Take dough from the far top edge, stretch gently toward you over center, press in.
  Then near edge up over center, slight overlap. Alternate right/left, "sewing" the
  dough over center — each stitch builds internal tension (a "spine"). Gentle
  stretch, not a tear. Great for high-hydration dough.
- **Close to shape:**
  - **Batard:** after stitching, fold the top third to center, press; roll toward
    you tightly, catching + stretching the outer layer each roll (like a rug); seal
    the seam.
  - **Boule:** gather all edges to center, pinch shut; flip seam-down, use circular
    motions + bench friction to round and tighten the surface.
  - Optional: drag the loaf on a bare/barely-floured bench to tighten the skin.
- **To banneton:** seam **up** (smooth side down) so the pretty side is up after
  the pre-bake flip. Flour the banneton well (rice flour ideal).
- Principles: tension not pressure; don't let it stick (tears the skin); enough
  flour to prevent sticking but not so much it blocks layers from sealing.

## Cold retard (התפחה ארוכה במקרר)

After final shaping, instead of a room-temp final proof, refrigerate.

- **Cover well** (cold fridge air is dry): shower cap, big plastic bag, cling film,
  towel, or silicone mat.
- **Fridge temp ideal 4–5 °C.**
- **Duration:** **8–12 h minimum**, up to 24, 48, even 72 h depending on recipe,
  starter strength, and desired sourness.
- **Why:** maximum bake-time flexibility (bake any convenient hour next day or 2–3
  days later, straight from the fridge). Splits the process across days. Cold dough
  is also easier to score.

### Enzyme science (the "why" behind long ferments)
- **Amylase** breaks starch → simple sugars (alpha cuts long chains to dextrins;
  beta nibbles ends releasing maltose) → feeds yeast + bacteria.
- **Protease** breaks gluten proteins → amino acids + peptides → extensibility +
  nitrogen for the microbes.
- Long cold ferment lets enzymes work over time → richer flavor (Maillard
  precursors), more open/soft crumb, deeper crust color, longer shelf life.
- Potential health angles: pre-digestion of starch/protein; partial gluten
  breakdown (not gluten-free) possibly easier for non-celiac sensitivity; **phytase**
  breaks phytic acid → better mineral absorption; lower glycemic index; more B
  vitamins + antioxidants. (Research still developing.)

## Baking principles (עקרונות אפייה)

Three heat-transfer modes in a home oven:
- **Radiation** — elements radiate heat; drives initial browning + crust.
- **Convection** — moving hot air (natural or fan) for even bake.
- **Conduction** — hot surface → loaf base; critical early.

**Two pillars of a good bake:**
1. **Strong bottom conduction** — an initial heat blast from below flashes dough
   water to steam → oven spring; without it the loaf is flat + dense, pale damp
   base. Preheat the baking surface hot.
2. **Steamy environment (first 15–20 min)** — keeps the surface moist + flexible so
   the loaf keeps expanding before the crust sets; gelatinizes surface starch for a
   glossy crisp crust; humid air transfers heat better. Then remove steam to dry +
   crisp + brown.

General: preheat thoroughly (walls + any baking surface must store energy); start
hot + humid, then lower humidity (and often temp) to bake through + crisp; learn
your specific oven (uneven heat); observe + log.

### Method 1 — Dutch oven (סיר ברזל יצוק)
High thermal mass = "oven inside the oven": even radiant heat all around, strong
base conduction, and **traps the dough's own moisture** (no external steam needed).
- **Preheat empty (pot + lid) 45–60 min at 230–250 °C.**
- Turn dough onto parchment (smooth side up), score, lower in via parchment "sling",
  cover immediately.
- **Covered 20–25 min** (steam trapped → oven spring + soft crust forming).
- Remove lid, **lower temp to 190–210 °C**, bake **15–30 min** more to brown + set.
- Done: deep amber crust, hollow tap, internal **96–99 °C**.
- Cool on a rack **1 h min, ideally 2–3 h** before slicing.
- Size the pot to the loaf (some clearance, not too much). Parchment eases transfer.

### Method 2 — Baking steel + lava rocks (פלדה + אבני טוף)
Steel conducts ~18–20× faster than ceramic stone → fierce base heat + spring.
Steel makes no steam; lava rocks in a metal tray make the steam.
- **Lava rocks** (porous volcanic): big surface area → fast intense steam, and safe
  (ordinary rocks can explode from trapped moisture).
- Steel on a center rack; rock tray on a lower rack/floor. **Preheat 45–60 min at
  230–250 °C, top+bottom heat, NO fan.**
- Slide scored dough onto the hot steel via peel/parchment.
- Pour **½–1 cup hot water** onto the rocks, shut the door fast (beware steam burst).
- **Steam phase 15–20 min**, then vent the door / optionally remove the rock tray,
  **lower to 190–210 °C**, bake **15–30 min** more.
- Done internal **96–99 °C**. Cool on rack 1 h min, 2–3 h ideal.
- Pros vs dutch oven: more even all-around crust, fits longer/multiple loaves,
  better visibility, less heavy-hot-vessel handling.

### Method 3 — Two loaf tins "sandwich" (תבנית אינגליש קייק)
Two identical loaf tins: one holds dough, the second inverted as a lid → small
semi-sealed chamber mimicking a dutch oven with common kitchenware. Softer crust
option, good for sandwich loaves.
- Flexible preheat: commonly do the **final proof in the base tin** (greased or
  parchment-lined, until nearly doubled), and only preheat the oven (+ optionally
  the lid tin). Or preheat both empty for a crisper base (harder transfer).
- Preheat oven **30–45 min, 200–230 °C**.
- Optional: brush top with water/milk + seeds; scoring optional for tin loaves.
- **Cover with the inverted tin, bake 15–25 min** (steam trapped, gains volume).
- Remove the lid tin, bake **15–30 min** more uncovered until browned through; tent
  with foil if the top darkens too fast.
- Done internal **90–96 °C** (soft) to **96–98 °C** (firmer). **Remove from the tin
  immediately** (trapped steam makes the base soggy). Cool ≥1 h.
- Light metal tins → faster bake, paler crust; dark tins → darker crisper crust.

## Baker's percentages (אחוזי אופה)

- **Flour (total) = 100%.** Every other ingredient = its weight ÷ total flour × 100.
- Hydration = water % relative to flour. Salt typically ~2%.
- **Pre-ferment** (levain/biga/poolish): its flour + water count toward the totals
  for hydration math; the pre-ferment % refers to its share of total flour.
- Use for scaling, comparing recipes, consistency, and understanding ingredient
  roles. *Example:* 1000 g flour @ 65% water = 650 g; 2.2% salt = 22 g.

## Switching flours: white → whole (שינוי קמחים)

- **Hydration:** whole flour absorbs more. Add **~8–10% of the added whole-flour
  weight** in extra water (range 5–15 g per 100 g swapped), adjust by feel.
  *Example:* swap 400 g to whole at 8% → +32 g water.
- **Fermentation:** whole flour (esp. rye) has more nutrients + enzymes → faster
  ferment, **shorter proof.** Optionally cut leavening ~5–10% or proof a bit cooler.
  *(Reconciled in [`timing-model.md` §3](./timing-model.md) flour model.)*
- Texture/flavor: denser, less airy, nutty/richer. Autolyse helps soften bran.
- Beginners: start ~25–30% whole, increase gradually.

## Final-proof graph (oven-entry timing)

Illustrates last proof before the oven: **room-temp dough** peaks ~5–6 h (narrow
window); **fridge dough** peaks ~18 h with a **much wider** acceptable window. The
"optimal oven entry" line sits at ~170% volume; fridge dough stays near it from
~6 h to ~46 h. Assumes an optimal bulk preceded it. This wide window is what makes
the retard the schedule's flexibility lever.
